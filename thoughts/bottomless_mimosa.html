On Bottomless Mimosas

Introduction

Picture yourself attending a pleasantly cosmopolitan brunch with your colleagues. At the center of the sophisticated gathering sits the beloved mimosa, a beautifully simple construction consisting of orange juice and champagne.1 You are caressed by the soft summer breeze, and the midday sun bestows the cool, inviting beverages with a resplendent shine. This occasion appears perfectly civilized until the server announces that the mimosas are bottomless.

With the introduction of this single word, the tranquility of the brunch is immediately ruined. No longer may we delight in the delicious and simple construction of the mimosa. We must now confront serious questions concerning the meaning of the bottomless mimosa. Establish a rigorous framework for understanding the bottomless mimosa.

On the Infinite Mimosa Fluid Assumption

An important question is whether our definition of a bottomless mimosa requires an infinite amount of mimosa liquid. Is it possible for a reasonable container to hold an infinite amount of liquid?

Definition.

A container is reasonable if it satisfies the following three properties:

  1. It can be served to guests by a server of finite strength without forcing the server to sacrifice their stylish and composed demeanor.
  2. It is composed of a finite amount of material, such as glass.
  3. It can be displayed inside a glass cabinet.

Relatively tame assumptions about our capabilities—such as having no access to spacetime-bending wormholes, portals, or similar technologies—reveal that a glass containing an infinite amount of mimosa fluid, no matter how clever its geometrical design, would not fit inside any room,2 the observable universe, or whatever brunch venue one chooses.

Claim 1.

Let $\mathfrak{F}\subset\mathbb{R}^3$ indicate the mimosa fluid belonging to a filled container, and let $\operatorname{Vol}(\mathfrak{F})$ refer to its volume in liters. If

$$ \operatorname{Vol}(\mathfrak{F})=\infty, $$

then there exists no bounding ball $B_R$ of finite radius $R$ containing $\mathfrak{F}$.

A little explanation is in order. The picture we have in mind is that $\mathfrak{F}$ is the contiguous region of space corresponding to the liquid in our container. In the case of a Collins glass, $\mathfrak{F}$ would be a tall, tapered cylinder. For a BuzzBallz-style container, $\mathfrak{F}$ would be approximately a small solid sphere.

Note that the preceding claim is agnostic regarding the particular geometry of the drinking glass. If our definition of a bottomless mimosa stipulates an infinite amount of liquid, then the mimosa glass cannot be reasonable.

Alternative Approaches to the Bottomless Ideal

Given the infeasibility of an infinite quantity of mimosa fluid, we now describe alternative constructions that approach the “bottomless” ideal.

The Half-Volume Dispensing Construction

Let us now concern ourselves with the colloquial, common-sense definition of a bottomless mimosa: a mimosa that one can continue drinking without ever running out.

Although we have shown that it is impossible to hold an infinite volume of mimosa in a finite container, we will now show how any local brunch establishment can provide its clientele with a never-ending mimosa-drinking experience.

Let us begin with a finite amount of glass from which to produce our drinking vessel. For simplicity, we idealize the mimosa glass as a hollow cube with finite internal volume.

The cube is filled with an initial volume $V_0$ of mimosa and equipped with a dispensing mechanism. Each time the drinker requests a serving, the mechanism dispenses exactly one half of the mimosa currently remaining in the glass.

This mechanism saves us from the need to model the volume of the drinker’s mouth, the duration of each sip, or any complicated fluid dynamics introduced by ordinary human drinking.

Before proving that this construction provides indefinitely many servings, we establish the following elementary result.

Lemma 1.

For every finite natural number $n\in\mathbb{N}$, the quantity $2^n$ is finite.

Proof.

We proceed by mathematical induction.

For the base case, let $n=0$. Then

$$ 2^0=1, $$

which is finite.

Now assume that $2^k$ is finite for some finite natural number $k\in\mathbb{N}$. Then

$$ 2^{k+1}=2\cdot 2^k. $$

The product of two finite real numbers is finite. Since $2$ is finite and $2^k$ is finite by the induction hypothesis, it follows that $2^{k+1}$ is also finite.

□
Claim 2.

A finite glass equipped with the half-volume dispensing mechanism can provide infinitely many nonzero servings of mimosa without becoming empty after any finite number of servings.

Proof.

Let $V_0>0$ denote the initial volume of mimosa in the glass. After the first serving, one half of the original volume is dispensed, leaving

$$ \frac{V_0}{2}. $$

After the second serving, one half of the remaining volume is dispensed, leaving

$$ \frac{V_0}{4}. $$

Continuing in this manner, after $n$ servings the volume remaining in the glass is

$$ V_n=\frac{V_0}{2^n}. $$

Since $V_0$ is positive and $2^n$ is finite and positive for every finite integer $n$ by Lemma 1, it follows that

$$ V_n>0 $$

for every finite number of servings. Thus, no matter how many servings have already been dispensed, some nonzero quantity of mimosa remains available for the next serving.

After $n$ servings, the total volume dispensed is

$$ V_0\left(1-\frac{1}{2^n}\right). $$

As $n$ increases without bound, this quantity approaches $V_0$. However, the remaining volume reaches zero only in the limit and not after any finite serving.

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}V_n = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{V_0}{2^n} = 0. $$

Since a human is presumably capable of requesting only finitely many servings at any finite moment in time, the glass never becomes empty during the drinking process. Therefore, the proposed mechanism produces a bottomless mimosa.

□

Appendix A: Omitted Proofs

Proof of Claim 1

Proof.

Assume, for the purpose of contradiction, that $\mathfrak{F}$ is contained in a ball $B_R$ of radius $R$, where $R$ is finite.

By monotonicity of volume,

$$ \mathfrak{F}\subseteq B_R \implies \operatorname{Vol}(\mathfrak{F}) \leq \operatorname{Vol}(B_R). \tag{1} $$

By elementary geometry,

$$ \operatorname{Vol}(B_R) = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3. $$

Since $R$ is finite, $\frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$ is also finite. Therefore, Equation (1) gives

$$ \operatorname{Vol}(\mathfrak{F}) \leq \operatorname{Vol}(B_R) = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 < \infty. $$

This contradicts the assumption that $\operatorname{Vol}(\mathfrak{F})=\infty$. Therefore, there is no finite-radius ball containing $\mathfrak{F}$.

□
